Drive southwest enough from any point in Europe and you will find Sagres, a centenary portugese village where the sea is still king.
Its location, surrounded by letal high cliffs and strong sea currents, is place to one of Portugal's most important militar stronghold, Fortaleza de Sagres.
But that's not what I decided to capture with my lens. Instead I focused on other life that you can find around Sagres. A centuries old lighthouse, people adventuring through life daring cliffs. Uniquely shaped rocky formations fighting the sea right below them, and humans that make the sea and what's whithin it, their payroll.
Mar Vivo, or Living Sea, is my way of presenting a lifestyle that has been around for milleniums, fishermanship. I decided to start the video with the opening scene of a lighthouse. A building that guides boats to safety seemed to me like a great way to start a video that would later showcase said fisherman's boats tied up at the docks, their safe place.
The lighthouse that you see here is the Farol do Cabo de S. Vicente, from there, you can observe the next two shots: Two imposing cliffs, one easily reachable by foot, where people can be seen gathering and admiring the view, and the other one empty due to its hard-to-reach, and dangerously steep location.
Slowly, we start descending our view into the sea that is dozens of meters below us. The first shot's background is only sky, the second shot shows a roughly fifthy percent ratio between sky and sea, and finally the third shot doesn't have any sky on it, only sea. Then, finally, our first time looking at the sea on the frontground.
It's during this fourth shot that we start seeing movement, a concept that will persist until the end of the piece, with exception to some shots deliberately still. During this scene, you can finally testify the nature of the sea that you knew was all around us, but haven't yet had the chance to experience.
For the next shot, we are no longer near the Farol. We moved like seaguls, from the sea to the docks. The next sights are tools of man. Objects that represent the work that is done on the docks. Traps for fish and crab, pallets to move the catch. An ordinary sight to the ones that use everyday but a delightful pattern for the ones that look for it.
Then, and finally, we see it. The one tool that makes fishing at sea what it is: Boats. We start with rudimental ones, simple boats that can carry four, maybe five, fishermans and respective tools. These are not meant for the open sea, they are the auxiliar boats that take our fisherman to the bigger vessel waiting to sail into the openness of the Atlantic.
And it's now, during the last third of the video, that we start witnessing a part of the craftsmanship. A man cleaning up his boat, a group untangling their nets.
